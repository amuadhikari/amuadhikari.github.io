Deforestation in Amazon

(Academic Research- group)

The research study focused on factors causing deforestation in the Amazon Forest and its consequences in the past 20 years. Proposals and future action plans were formulated for its reforestation based on several outcomes like estimated loss of trees, regrowth, time, and budget for plantation of new trees, role of local communities, available technology, and concept of sustainable forestry. During the research, we also interacted with many global agents working for the protection of the Amazon Forest and gained valuable insights about the global issue.

Spread across eight countries - Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Bolivia, Guyana, Ecuador, Suriname, and French Guiana - the Amazon represents nearly a third of all tropical rainforest. At present, nearly 20 million forest land is lost due to deforestation, which means that 2400 trees are cut every minute. It is accounted that agriculture is responsible for 80% of forest cover loss. Due to this about 137 different species of the ecosystem and 1400 species of indigenous trees are critically endangered. It is believed that at this current rate entire forest lands would be lost in around 70 years. Apart from this, the Amazon Forest also acts as the ‘cloud house’ of the planet and helps in regulating the water cycle. It also acts as a major natural source of medicines with nearly 25% of cancer curing drugs coming from rainforests. The Amazon is also home to 400 indigenous communities who have lived here for more than 30,000 years and rely on the forest for food and shelter. Deforestation thus also poses an indirect threat to the indigenous culture and heritage. Clearing of forest cover also increases the risk of drought and flood occurrences. The Amazon Forest also acts as a carbon sink and absorbs one-fourth of the CO2 absorbed by all the land on earth. The amount absorbed today, however, is 30% less than it was in the 1990s because of deforestation. It is estimated that deforesting 20% of the Amazon could push it past a ‘tipping point’, triggering a large-scale dieback that would release more than 90 billion tonnes of CO2 into the atmosphere. .
