Border conflicts and climate change

(Academic Project)

This is a studio project from the second semester at Virginia Tech. Through this project global issues of climate change in a region of great territorial conflict were resolved using strategies that are beneficial to humanity and the ecosystem. The design solution in the form of an artistic installation/statement advocates about politics along the conservative/liberal spectrum giving way to a new earth - directed politics dedicated to preserving, enhancing, and desacralizing life within the biosphere.

The site was chosen at the tip of Siachen glacier in the Himalayas. The glacier is the world’s highest battlefield at an elevation of about 18,000’ above sea level. The Siachen glacier is guarded by the military forces of China in the East, India in the south and Pakistan in the west and is often a reason for conflict among these nations over territory, which leads to the breaking of ceasefire at the borders. The 75km long glacier is just one of many Himalayan glaciers which is melting at unprecedented rates. These glaciers could soon lose up to 75% of their volume by the century's end due to global warming. This could lead to flooding and water shortages for the nearly 2 billion people who live downstream of the rivers that originate in the mountainous region.

The design draws inspiration from the works of eminent artists and activists from around the globe like Kristo and Jeane Claude, Robert Smithson, James Turell, Olafur Eliason etc. The design involves two components, first, the 50m(dia.) solar powered inflatable structures that shade the tip of the glacier by casting a shadow and second, a 100m long nylon fabric that shields the glacier from sunrays during the summer months. The artistic installation symbolizes a point of unity brought together by a greater conscience for the protection of the planet, at a location which was otherwise known as a battlefield. Additionally, a viewing deck with a helipad was constructed to facilitate the tourism on a glacier with extreme climatic conditions. The structure and form of the inflated structures derives inspiration from available research and technology developed by NASA(https://www.science.org/content/article/commercial-balloons-stratosphere-could-monitor-hurricanes-and-scan-solar-storms), architect Joseph Cory (https://inhabitat.com/sunhope-solar-balloons/) and MIT research (https://news.mit.edu/2022/ultrathin-solar-cells-1209).
